Why Analyse Images?

Visual Texts

Photographs are visual texts — just like a poem or novel, they communicate meaning through deliberate choices.

Hidden Messages

Every image carries a point of view. Photographers choose what to shoot, when, and how — nothing is neutral.

Critical Thinking

Analysing images builds the same skills needed for any text: observation, inference, and evidence-based argument.

Use these steps every time you analyse a photograph

You will use these 5 steps with real photos today — have your analysis worksheet ready.

1
Observe

What do
you see?

Before you guess what is happening — just describe what is literally in front of you.

  • 👥Who or what is in the image? Describe the people, objects, animals.
  • 📍Where are they? Describe the setting and background.
  • 🎨What colours, lighting, and mood can you see?
  • 🔍What is in the foreground? What is in the background?
  • 📷Is anything unusual, unexpected, or out of place?
2
Question

What do
you wonder?

Good analysts ask questions — not just about what they see, but about what they don't know.

  • ❓What is happening that you cannot explain yet?
  • 🗓️When do you think this photograph was taken?
  • 🌍Where in the world might this be?
  • 📸Who do you think took this photo — and why?
  • 😶What are the people in the image thinking or feeling?
3
Context

What is the
context?

Context is everything that surrounds the image — the time, place, and social situation it was taken in.

💡 Use your prior knowledge first — then your teacher will reveal the context.
  • 🗺️Where was this taken?
  • 🏛️What social issues are shown?
  • 📰Does this connect to a real event?
  • 🕰️What year or era is it from?
  • 👤Who published or commissioned it?
  • 🎯Who was the intended audience?
4
Conclude

What is
actually
happening?

Combine your observations, questions, and context to build an evidence-based interpretation.

  • 🔗How does what you see connect to the wider context?
  • ⚖️What evidence supports your interpretation? Be specific.
  • 🤔Could there be another reading? What might you be missing?
  • 📊How reliable is this source? Could it be staged, cropped, or edited?
  • 🗣️What language would you use to write about this formally?
Step 5 — Message

What is the photographer saying?

Moving beyond description to interpretation and written argument.

Framing

What is included — and excluded — from the shot?

Perspective

High angle = power over subject. Low angle = subject looks powerful.

Light & Shadow

Bright light = hope or heroism. Darkness = threat or despair.

Composition

What is centred? What is pushed to the edge?

The photographer wants the viewer to feel / think / understand because .

Share & Debate Your Findings

Class Discussion

Share your Observation

What did different groups notice? What surprised you about what others saw?

Compare Questions

Did others ask questions you hadn't thought of? What questions are still unanswered?

Debate the Message

Did everyone reach the same conclusion about the photographer's intent? Why or why not?

There is no single 'correct' answer — historians and critics debate interpretation. If everyone agrees, we probably haven't looked hard enough.
Independent Task

Writing
Task

Write a structured image analysis of 8–12 sentences.

Use your worksheet notes  ·  Full sentences only  ·  15 minutes

§1 — Observe

Describe what you can see in the image. Include people, objects, setting, mood, and composition. Use precise vocabulary.

§2 — Context

Explain the context surrounding the image. When and where was this taken? What situation or issue does it relate to?

§3 — Conclude

State what you believe is actually happening in the image. Use specific visual evidence from the photograph to support your claim.

§4 — Message

Explain the photographer's intended message and effect on the viewer. Use language techniques — framing, light, angle, symbolism — as evidence.

What we learned

Key
Takeaways

📸

Photographs are visual texts — they communicate meaning just like written texts.

🔍

Always observe first, then question, then seek context.

🧠

Context changes how we read and interpret an image.

✍️

Back your interpretation with specific visual evidence and language.

Homework

Find your own historical photograph from the NYT archive or a history website. Apply all 5 steps in writing. Bring your analysis to the next lesson — be ready to share.
